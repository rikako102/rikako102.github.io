I studied Furniture Design while abroad in Copenhagen, Denmark

[September - December 2017]


The Lagkage rocking chair is a study of light in curves and white spaces.

Lagkage is Danish for "layer cake". Traditionally, it's served for birthdays and special occasions, often with lots of Danish flags stuck in them.

I chose this name for my chair because of the layered slats that make up the backrest, but also to remind me of the incredible cultural experiences I had living as a Dane with my host family.

Instructor: Erling Christoffersen

Guidelines for the build:

  • Made from bamboo plywood, a sustainable and low impact material
  • Disassembles into flat-pack pieces with minimal hardware, like IKEA furniture
  • Cannot exceed 60 x 60 x 60 inches

Obviously, Scandinavian design was a huge inspiration.

In particular, Hans Wegner's "The Round Chair", Alvar Aalto's Paimio Sanatorium, and Alejandro Castro's R Sofa played a huge role in my brainstorming. I also knew from early on that I wanted to design a rocking chair for the challenge and creative potential.

Once I had a favorite design, I constructed a full-scale cardboard mockup.



Some key designs to note:

  • A backrest of slats extending above and below the armrest...
  • ...which, fully inspired by Wegner, extends from a backrest
  • Rockers that angle inward for stability
  • A broad seat, ~110 degrees off the back, for maximum comfort

At Erling's suggestion, I entertained the thought of getting rid of the top two slats. That way, the long armrest becomes the focal point, and simplifies the overall design.

One major issue I ran into was the curvature of the rockers.

It depends on the weight and center of mass of the rest of the chair, so I built several full-size rocker feet out of particle chip board to test. To not feel like you were going to tip over backwards every time, a fairly flat curve was needed.

After some design iterations, I began building.

A design choice I made during the build was the lengths of the wooden slats. In its final form, Lagkage has four slats; the one closest to the seat is shorter than the one above it, and so on until the fully extended armrest at the top.

There are only 10 separate parts in this chair. The only hardware needed in constructing Lagkage is brass t-screws; everything else is snugly fit together.



This chair currently resides in California, waiting to be transported to a more permanent home.


Fun fact: The furniture program almost didn't happen.

Enrollment for Fall 2017 was incredibly low - just 2 people - and the study abroad program was about to cut it. I sent an empassioned email explaining why this couldn't happen, and I convinced them. It was a cozy semester with a lot of one-on-one instructor attention and bonding with my only classmate.

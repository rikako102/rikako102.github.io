I worked in the Interiors division, which dealt with everything on the interior of the car: dashboard, seats, sun visor, cup holders, etc.

My main role was putting out fires; I found recurring problems on the assembly line and did a root cause analysis to solve them.


One major problem I solved was air vents that rattled and jammed during inspection.

To find the root cause, I began by observing installation on the manufacturing line. On 30 random Model X's, I compiled data on gap tolerances, force efforts, and a percentage of defective parts.

From this, I found two problems:
  • A panel in the air vent was not aligning properly, which created the clicking noise
  • Incorrect installation techniques were used on the assembly line, which probably made the manufacturer defects worse.




This created two opportunities for improvement.


On the supplier side, I set up a meeting to talk about my findings and discuss quality of parts and manufacturing. However, because supplier improvements can take weeks to take effect, I created a temporary countermeasure in the meantime that was implemented on the production floor.

On the assembly side, I rewrote the work instructions in greater detail to decrease chance of error in installation. I reviewed them with the line managers, who were responsible for passing along the instructions to the night shift.

The rate of error decreased by 50% within 3 days of implementing changes. When reworked parts were introduced, the error frequency was 0-1%, which is within the acceptable range.

Is that all you did at Tesla?

Not at all! My other tasks included reviewing CATIA files for the Model 3, which was not in production yet at the time, fixing tolerances in door-to-body connections, and performing a similar process with the air vents on misaligning instrument panels.
